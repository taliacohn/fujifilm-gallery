üìù How to Set Film Simulation Recipes on Your X-T3

Method 1: Using Custom Settings (C1-C7)

  1. Go to MENU ‚Üí SHOOTING SETTING ‚Üí EDIT/SAVE CUSTOM SETTING
  2. Select one of the custom slots (C1-C7)
  3. Adjust all the settings below for your recipe
  4. Save the settings to that custom slot
  5. Switch to it anytime using the top dial (rotate to C1-C7)

Method 2: Quick Setup Without Saving

  1. Press Q button for Quick Menu
  2. Navigate to each setting and adjust:
    • Film Simulation
    • Dynamic Range
    • White Balance
    • Highlight/Shadow
    • Color
    • Sharpness
    • Noise Reduction
üí° Pro Tip: Save your favorite recipes to C1-C7 so you can quickly switch between them while shooting!

‚öôÔ∏è Recipe Settings Explained

Dynamic Range

Options: DR100, DR200, DR400, Auto

What it does: Preserves highlights and shadows. Higher values (DR400) prevent blown highlights in bright scenes but require higher ISO (minimum ISO 800 for DR400).

When to use: Use DR400 in high-contrast scenes (bright sky + dark foreground). DR100 for normal contrast.

Grain Effect

Options: Off, Weak, Strong

What it does: Adds film-like grain texture to images.

When to use: Weak for subtle film look, Strong for vintage aesthetic.

Color Chrome Effect

Options: Off, Weak, Strong

What it does: Deepens colors in highly saturated areas without affecting less saturated areas.

When to use: Strong for rich, deep colors (sunsets, flowers). Can make colors look "muddy" in some situations.

White Balance

Options: Auto, Daylight, Shade, Fluorescent, Incandescent, Kelvin (2500K-10000K), Custom R/B shifts

What it does: Sets color temperature. Red (+R) makes warmer, Blue (+B) makes cooler.

Common settings:

  • Daylight: 5200K - outdoor sunny scenes
  • Shade: ~7000K - cooler, for shade
  • 3200K: Tungsten/indoor lights
  • Custom R/B shifts: Fine-tune (e.g., "2R -4B" = +2 Red, -4 Blue)

Highlight

Range: -2 to +4

What it does: Adjusts brightness of highlights (bright areas).

When to use: Negative values (-2) soften highlights, positive values (+2) increase contrast.

Shadow

Range: -2 to +4

What it does: Adjusts brightness of shadows (dark areas).

When to use: Positive values (+2) lift shadows, negative values deepen them.

Color

Range: -4 to +4

What it does: Overall color saturation.

When to use: Negative values for muted/faded look, positive values for vibrant colors.

Sharpness

Range: -4 to +4

What it does: Edge sharpness and detail.

When to use: Negative values (-2) for softer, film-like look. Positive for crisp details.

Noise Reduction

Range: -4 to +4

What it does: Reduces noise in high ISO images.

When to use: -4 preserves grain/texture (film look), +4 smooths noise at high ISO.

üìê The Exposure Triangle

Three settings control your exposure. Adjusting one requires adjusting another to maintain the same brightness:

üîÜ ISO (Sensitivity)

Range: 160-12800 (expandable to 80-51200)

What it does: Sensor sensitivity to light

  • Low ISO (160-400): Clean, detailed images. Use in bright light.
  • Medium ISO (800-3200): Good balance. Indoor/overcast.
  • High ISO (6400+): More noise, but needed in dark conditions.

Trade-off: Higher ISO = more noise/grain

‚è±Ô∏è Shutter Speed

Range: 30 seconds to 1/8000 sec

What it does: How long sensor is exposed to light

  • Fast (1/500+): Freeze motion (sports, action)
  • Medium (1/60-1/250): General photography
  • Slow (1/60 or slower): Motion blur, need tripod

Trade-off: Slower shutter = more motion blur

Rule of thumb: Keep shutter speed ‚â• 1/focal length (e.g., 1/50 for 50mm lens)

‚≠ï Aperture (f-stop)

Range: Depends on lens (e.g., f/1.4 - f/16)

What it does: Size of lens opening

  • Wide (f/1.4-f/2.8): More light, shallow depth of field (blurry background)
  • Medium (f/4-f/8): Balanced sharpness
  • Narrow (f/11-f/16): Less light, everything in focus (landscapes)

Trade-off: Wide aperture = shallow focus. Narrow = deeper focus.

Remember: Smaller f-number = wider opening = more light!

üí° Quick Reference

For portraits: Low ISO (160-400), wide aperture (f/1.4-f/2.8), fast enough shutter (1/125+)

For landscapes: Low ISO (160-400), narrow aperture (f/8-f/16), tripod for slow shutter

For street/action: Auto ISO (up to 3200), fast shutter (1/500+), medium aperture (f/4-f/8)

For low light: High ISO (1600-6400), wide aperture (f/1.4-f/2.8), slow shutter (with stabilization)

‚ú® Tips & Tricks for X-T3

Shoot RAW+JPEG

Set to RAW+JPEG so you can experiment with recipes in-camera (JPEG) while keeping RAW files for editing flexibility.

Use Film Simulation Bracketing

Set up bracketing to shoot 3 different film simulations with one press. Great for testing recipes!

Preview Your Recipe

EVF/LCD shows you the recipe in real-time! What you see is what you get (for JPEG).

Test Different Lighting

Same recipe can look very different in golden hour vs. midday. Use tags to track this!

Start with Classic Chrome

Most recipes use Classic Chrome as a base. It's versatile and forgiving.

Adjust White Balance First

White balance has the biggest impact on the recipe's look. Nail this first!
